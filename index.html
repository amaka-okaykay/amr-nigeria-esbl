CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE — 90.8% CEFTAZIDIME RESISTANCE

Ceftazidime is a third-generation cephalosporin and a primary empirical treatment for gram-negative infections in Nigerian hospitals. A 90.8% resistance rate means the antibiotic most likely to be prescribed is ineffective in 9 out of 10 culture-confirmed cases. This finding has direct, urgent implications for clinical prescribing guidelines and AMR control policy in Imo State tertiary hospitals.

03 Multi-Gene Co-Resistance Patterns
Co-occurrence of Resistance Gene Families
% of ESBL+ isolates (n=92) carrying each gene combination — clinical co-resistance is the dominant pattern
Clinical Implication
54.3% of confirmed ESBL isolates carry 2 or more gene families simultaneously — meaning combined resistance mechanisms, not single-gene carriage, is the dominant pattern in this clinical environment.
Most Common Pattern
blaCTX-M + blaTEM co-occurrence is the most frequent multi-gene pattern (38.0%), consistent with plasmid-mediated horizontal gene transfer as the primary dissemination mechanism.
Surveillance Gap
PCR confirms presence of gene families. Only WGS-based analysis can determine exact gene variants (e.g. blaCTX-M-15 vs blaCTX-M-1) and trace plasmid transmission networks across hospitals.

Why this matters globally: Africa's genomic data is critically underrepresented in every major AMR database. WHO GLASS, NCBI, and CARD are dominated by European and North American sequences. Nigeria — with 14,165 hospitals and one of the highest burdens of drug-resistant infections in sub-Saharan Africa — contributes almost no WGS-confirmed AMR data to these repositories. This research pipeline is designed to change that.
